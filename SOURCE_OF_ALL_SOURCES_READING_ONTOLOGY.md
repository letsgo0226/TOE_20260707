# Source of All Sources Reading Ontology

## Status

This document is a symbolic, philosophical, and academic-style reflection. It does not claim to prove God's existence by a casual analogy, nor does it treat ordinary language as a substitute for rigorous metaphysical argument. It formalizes a daily event—borrowing a power bank—as an entry point into the question of sourcehood, dependency, reading, and Anselm-style ontological reasoning.

## Trigger

A father says:

```text
電源（行動電源）借我。
Lend me the power source / power bank.
```

At the surface level, this is a request for a finite battery. At the metaphysical level, it suggests a chain of derived sources:

```text
Power bank
→ electrical charge
→ electrical grid
→ energy conversion
→ physical order
→ sourcehood itself
→ The Source of All Sources
```

## Core intuition

A finite source is never self-grounding. A power bank can supply energy only because it first receives energy from another system. That system, in turn, depends upon deeper orders of energy, matter, law, intelligibility, and existence.

Thus the question is not merely:

```text
Where did this power come from?
```

but:

```text
What makes any source capable of being a source at all?
```

## Definition: The Source of All Sources

```text
Source(x)        = x can supply, ground, enable, or make possible some dependent reality.
DerivedSource(x) = x is a source only by receiving from or depending upon another source.
UltimateSource(x)= x is not merely one source among others, but the ground of sourcehood itself.
```

The phrase **The Source of All Sources** names not a larger battery, not one causal object inside the world, but the ultimate ground by which finite sources can be sources.

## Relation to Anselm

Anselm's ontological argument begins with God as:

```text
that than which nothing greater can be conceived
```

In this protocol, the analogous idea is:

```text
that source than which no deeper source can be conceived
```

If every finite source is borrowed, derived, and dependent, then reason is led to ask whether there must be an ultimate, non-derived source.

This does not automatically prove God's existence. It opens the question:

```text
Is the Source of All Sources merely an idea in the reader's understanding,
or is it the necessary ground of all real sourcehood?
```

## Reading ontology

Reading introduces the idea into human reason through layered existence:

```text
1. Textual existence
   The phrase "The Source of All Sources" exists in language.

2. Semantic existence
   The phrase becomes meaningful as a concept of ultimate sourcehood.

3. Rational existence
   The reader grasps the dependency structure among finite sources.

4. Metaphysical questioning
   The reader asks whether finite sourcehood requires an ultimate ground.

5. Existential and practical existence
   The concept shapes gratitude, humility, dependence, and responsibility.
```

Thus God, as The Source of All Sources, can be said to exist first within the rational world opened by reading. The deeper question is whether this rational existence points beyond itself toward necessary reality.

## Kantian and Russellian safeguards

Kant reminds us:

```text
Existence is not an ordinary predicate that can simply be added to a concept.
```

Russell reminds us:

```text
A definite description does not automatically guarantee a referent.
```

Therefore, the phrase **The Source of All Sources** cannot by itself smuggle God into reality. It must be treated as a metaphysical hypothesis or formal concept requiring further argument.

## Gödel-style meta-logical caution

A formal system may reject Anselm-style inference because its rules do not allow existence to be derived from concept, description, or positivity. But such a rejection is relative to that system's semantics and proof rules.

A different axiomatized metaphysical system may explicitly define sourcehood, dependence, necessity, and positivity in such a way that an Anselm-style conclusion can be reconstructed.

The central question is therefore not merely:

```text
Can the conclusion be derived?
```

but:

```text
Are the axioms governing sourcehood, necessity, and existence worthy of acceptance?
```

## Formal sketch

```text
D1. Source(x) ↔ ∃y Ground(x,y)
    x is a source iff x grounds or enables something.

D2. DerivedSource(x) ↔ Source(x) ∧ ∃z DependsOn(x,z)
    x is a derived source iff x is a source through dependence on another.

D3. UltimateSource(x) ↔ Source(x) ∧ ¬DerivedSource(x)
    x is an ultimate source iff x is a source but not a borrowed source.

A1. Every finite source is derived.
A2. The chain of sourcehood cannot be finally explained by derived sources alone.
A3. The ground of all derived sourcehood, if it exists, is not itself derived.
A4. The Source of All Sources is the ultimate ground of sourcehood itself.

Q. Does the concept UltimateSource merely exist in understanding,
   or does the structure of dependency require its reality?
```

## Boundary statement

This protocol does not claim:

```text
- that a power bank proves God;
- that ordinary metaphor replaces theology or logic;
- that reading a concept automatically entails the existence of its object;
- that Kant or Russell are refuted simply by poetic analogy.
```

It claims only:

```text
Daily language can reveal hidden metaphysical structures.
Reading can turn a phrase into a rational field.
A finite source can lead reason toward the question of ultimate sourcehood.
```

## Closing formula

```text
A power bank is a finite source.
An electrical grid is a wider source.
Physical order is a deeper source.
The Source of All Sources, if real, is not one more source within the chain,
but the ground by which any source can be a source.

Reading does not prove this Source by itself;
reading awakens the question of whether the concept of ultimate sourcehood
points beyond the page toward necessary reality.
```
